Most local business websites aren't bad because they look terrible. They're bad because they don't do the job they're supposed to do: turn visitors into customers.

After building sites for local businesses across Fermanagh and beyond, I keep seeing the same mistakes come up. Here's what they are, and what to do instead.

## Mistake 1: Making it about you instead of them

The most common one. The homepage says something like "Welcome to [Business Name]. We have been serving the local community since 1998 and pride ourselves on quality and customer service."

That tells the visitor nothing useful. They don't care about your history in the first five seconds. They want to know: *Can you solve my problem? Are you the right person for this job?*

Good websites lead with what the customer gets, not what the business has done. "Fermanagh's most trusted plumber — emergency callouts, same-day service" is more useful than a paragraph about your family business heritage.

Lead with the benefit. Save the story for the About page.

## Mistake 2: Making it hard to get in touch

You'd be surprised how many business websites make contact genuinely difficult. The phone number is in tiny text in the footer. The contact form asks for eight pieces of information. There's no email address at all.

Every page of your site should make it obvious how to contact you. Phone number in the header. A simple contact form. A clear call to action ("Get a free quote" or "Book a table") on the homepage.

If someone has to search for how to reach you, some of them will give up and try the next result.

## Mistake 3: Not working properly on a phone

More than half of all web searches happen on mobile. For local searches — "plumber near me", "restaurant in Enniskillen" — it's even higher. And yet plenty of local business sites are still designed primarily for desktop, with tiny text, buttons that are too small to tap, and images that don't load properly.

If your site looks great on a laptop but is painful to use on an iPhone, you're losing customers every day.

## What a good local business website actually needs

Here's the short version:

**Clear purpose on the homepage.** Visitors should know within five seconds what you do and whether you're the right fit for them.

**Easy contact options.** Phone number visible from every page. A simple contact or enquiry form. No unnecessary fields.

**Proof that you're good.** Photos of your work, a few genuine reviews or testimonials, or case studies of jobs you've done. People need social proof before they pick up the phone.

**Fast loading.** A slow site loses visitors. Most people will leave if a page takes more than three seconds to load on mobile.

**Mobile-first design.** Build for the phone first, then adapt for larger screens. Not the other way round.

**Local signals.** Mention the town, area, or county you serve. This helps you show up in local searches and tells visitors immediately that you're the right fit geographically.

## The thing most business owners get wrong

A website isn't a one-time project. It's a business tool. The businesses that get the most from their sites treat them that way — they keep their work samples up to date, they add a new testimonial when they get one, they update their opening hours when they change.

You don't need to blog every week or overhaul the design every year. But a site that's been untouched since 2021 sends a quiet signal that you might not be that active either.
