"SEO" sounds complicated. Agencies charge a fortune for it. There are entire books written about it. But for a local business — a shop, a tradesperson, a restaurant — the basics are actually pretty straightforward.

You don't need to understand how Google's algorithm works. You need to understand what your customers type when they're looking for someone like you.

## What local SEO actually means

Local SEO is just making it easier for people in your area to find your business when they search online. That's it.

When someone in Enniskillen searches "electrician near me" or "best coffee shop Fermanagh," Google decides which businesses to show based on a handful of factors. Getting those factors right is what local SEO is about.

## The single most important thing: your Google Business Profile

If you do nothing else, set up and fill out your Google Business Profile properly. It's free, and it directly affects whether you show up in local search results and on Google Maps.

Key things to fill in:

- **Business name** — exactly as it appears on your signage and website
- **Category** — be specific (e.g. "Plumber" not just "Home Services")
- **Address and service area** — where you're based and where you work
- **Phone number and website**
- **Opening hours** — and keep them updated
- **Photos** — at least a few good ones of your work, your premises, or your team
- **Reviews** — more on this below

An incomplete or inaccurate Google Business Profile is one of the most common reasons local businesses don't show up where they should.

## Reviews matter more than most people realise

Google gives prominence to businesses with more reviews and higher ratings. But it's not just about gaming the algorithm — real customers read reviews before deciding to contact you.

The best way to get more reviews is simple: ask. When you finish a job and the customer is happy, ask them if they'd mind leaving a Google review. Most people are glad to do it if they're satisfied — they just don't think to unless you ask.

Responding to reviews (good and bad) also signals to Google that you're an active, engaged business.

## Your website needs to mention where you are

This sounds obvious, but a lot of business websites never actually say where they're based. If your site doesn't mention "Fermanagh," "Enniskillen," or whatever area you serve, Google has a harder time connecting you with local searches.

Simple fixes:

- Include your town or county in your page title and description
- Mention your location naturally in your homepage copy ("Based in Enniskillen, serving across Fermanagh and beyond")
- Add your full address in the footer

You don't need to stuff your location into every sentence. Just make sure it's clearly present.

## Match what your customers actually type

Think about how your customers search for you. They're not typing technical terms — they're typing things like "plumber Enniskillen" or "wedding cake maker Fermanagh" or "hair salon open Saturday near me."

Your website copy should use the same natural language your customers use. Not jargon, not industry terms they wouldn't know — the plain words they'd type into Google when they need you.

## The basics checklist

Here's what most local businesses need to have in place:

- Google Business Profile set up and fully completed
- Website that loads fast and works on mobile
- Your location mentioned clearly on your website
- A handful of genuine Google reviews
- Consistent business name, address, and phone number across your website and Google profile

That's the foundation. If these aren't in place, no amount of advanced SEO work will help much.

## What about blogging and content?

Writing articles (like this one) can help you show up for more searches over time. But it's a long game, and for most local businesses, the basics above will have a much bigger impact in the short term.

Once the fundamentals are solid, adding the occasional helpful article relevant to your customers and your area is a worthwhile next step.
